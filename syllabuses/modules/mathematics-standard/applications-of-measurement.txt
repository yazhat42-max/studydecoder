Real-world applications
Considerations
    • The internet could be used as a source of suitable maps and plans for the estimation of distances.
    • Students could compare costs of different trade professionals using spreadsheets.
    • Where possible, students are encouraged to use a ruler to take measurements to create a scale and determine measurements using a scale.
    • Converting between rates involves converting between units of two different quantities, for example, length and time in rates such as km/h to m/s. Students may need to review conversion between units to support them when converting between rates.
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Priorities in Ratio and rates
    • Ratio is an important mathematical concept that is connected to customary and contemporary Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander maps, artworks and artefacts. By using ratio, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples can create representations of their lands, Cultures, Histories and stories that are meaningful, accurate and respectful.

Teaching advice for Network flow
Content
    § Network flow
Teaching advice
Purpose
Networks involve the graphical representation and modelling of situations as an approach to decision-making processes. Knowledge of networks enables development of a logical sequence of tasks or a clear understanding of connections between people or items.
Study of networks is important in developing students’ ability to interpret a set of connections or sequence of tasks as a concise diagram in order to solve related problems. Studying networks also develops students’ awareness of the applicability of networks throughout their lives (such as social networks) and their ability to use associated techniques to optimise practical problems.
Key terms

    • directed network
    • edge (networks)
    • flow capacity
    • inflow (networks)
    • maximum-flow minimum-cut theorem
    • minimum cut
    • outflow (networks)​
    • sink (networks)
    • source (networks)
Prior knowledge, skills and understanding
These concepts may be reviewed as required:
    • defining and using network terminology
    • constructing networks and solving related problems
    • determining minimum spanning trees
    • determining the shortest path in a given network.

Making connections
Connections
Course/Stage/Focus area
There are many applications of problems involving flows through networks, such as the movement of traffic through an airport, the flow of water in a system of pipes or the transfer of data across a computer intranet.
Real-world applications
Considerations
    • Students should be supported to develop fluency in converting information between different representations of networks, for example, converting from a table to a diagram and vice versa.
    • A flow must obey two rules: 
        ◦ The flow assigned to an edge must lie between zero and the capacity of the edge.
        ◦ The inflow of a vertex (total flow of incoming edges) must be equal to the outflow of a vertex (total flow of outgoing edges). This means that vertices cannot ‘store’ flow and are simply where edges meet.

Making connections
Connections
Course/Stage/Focus area
Critical path analysis can be applied in the field of medicine, such as in the scheduling of surgeries or clinical trials for drug development.
Real-world applications
Considerations
    • There are different ways to conduct a critical path analysis on a network diagram. This can include using one rectangle or circle for EST & LST at each vertex or using one rectangle or circle for EST & LST for each activity. Students should be encouraged to use an accurate method that they are confident in.
    • Students should explore the effect that delays in activities may have on the critical path.
    • Students should become familiar with converting information between different representations of networks, for example, converting from a table to a network diagram or Gantt chart and vice versa.
    • When using a spreadsheet to model a project, students should construct a table and either merge or shade cells to indicate an activity’s length.
