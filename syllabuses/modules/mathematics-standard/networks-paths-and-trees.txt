Teaching advice
Purpose
Networks involve the graphical representation and modelling of situations as an approach to decision-making processes. Knowledge of networks enables development of a logical sequence of tasks or a clear understanding of connections between people or items.
Study of networks is important in developing students’ ability to interpret a set of connections or sequence of tasks as a concise diagram in order to solve related problems. Studying networks also develops students’ awareness of the applicability of networks throughout their lives (such as social networks) and their ability to use associated techniques to optimise practical problems.

The study of networks can be continued in vocational contexts, for example, examining the efficiency of a delivery service or minimising costs of connecting services, and at the tertiary level, in areas such as project management, data analysis and scientific research.
Real-world applications
Analysing current social networks using technology is a relevant application of network theory and has become a vibrant field of research called social network analysis. Similar applications of network theory are found in many other areas, including linguistics, criminology, medicine and law.
Real-world applications
Considerations
    • Students should be exposed to a variety of algorithms for finding the minimum spanning tree and the shortest path. However, they are not required to demonstrate a specified algorithm.
    • Algorithms to determine the minimum spanning tree include Kruskal’s or Prim’s.
    • The standard algorithm to find the shortest path is Dijkstra’s algorithm. However, other algorithms to find the shortest path could be explored.
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Priorities in Networks, paths and trees
    • Songlines and star maps are networks that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples use to encode and share their Knowledge, values and wisdom about the land, the sea, the sky, and the culture. These networks also influence the modern Australian road network, such as the Kamilaroi Highway, which follows a Songline.
    • The 8 Aboriginal Ways of Learning is a network of eight pedagogies that use Aboriginal perspectives and techniques to teach and learn.
Diversity of learners
    • Teachers should consider the accessibility of tools and resources. Considerations for tools such as spreadsheets, calculators and applications may include compatibility with screen readers and other assistive technologies including alternative input devices and accessible calculators (see approved calculators). Considerations for resources may include the language demands, readability of and long descriptions for images, and providing alternative text for graphs and diagrams.
    • Relating mathematics learning to real-world contexts supports learning by providing concrete examples and establishing relevance to facilitate motivation and engagement. Manipulatives and real-world objects may reduce the language load for students.
    • Students with disability may require adjustments including access to visual or digital aids to communicate and demonstrate knowledge, understanding and skills.
    • Students for whom English is an additional language or dialect (EAL/D) may require additional support and targeted strategies to support their learning of language specific to mathematics.
    • The pace and complexity of learning may need to be adjusted for students who progress at different rates.
