• Explains effectively how Pippos portrays Lucky ’s sense of hope for the 
future, using well-chosen supporting evidence 4 
• Explains how Pippos portrays Lucky ’s sense of hope for the future , using 
appropriate supporting evidence 3 
• Describes how Pippos portrays Lucky ’s sense of hope for the future , 
using some supporting evidence 2 
• Provides some relevant information 1 
Sample answer: 
Pippos portrays Lucky ’s hope for the future through the metaphor of the ship as a ‘diorama of 
the world’, show ing that Lucky feels like the whole world is open to him. Lucky imagines Valia 
at various times of day, ‘working or eating ’, or ‘flicking up to distance a fly’, and Pippos states 
that for Lucky these are ‘pleasing scenarios ’. Lucky has ‘two gold rings hidden ’ which are 
symbolic of the love and promise of marriage and hope he feels for his future with Valia in 
Australia. Pippos describes Circular Qua y as ‘heavy with activity’ and the ‘northern shore in 
repose ’, the contrast between action and peace reflecting Lucky’s hope for his future success 
in both his business and his peaceful home with Valia .

 •            Explains in a highly effective manner how Fidge explores the impact of 
      role models in her childhood , using detailed, well -chosen supporting 
 evidence  5 
 •            Explains effectively how Fidge explores the impact of role models in her 
  childhood, using well -chosen supporting evidence  4 
 •            Explains how Fidge explores the impact of role models in her childhood , 
    with appropriate supporting evidence  3 
 •          Describes the impact of role models in her childhood  2 
 •     Provides some relevant information  1 
Sample answer: 
Fidge explores the ‘monumental ’ impact that her favourite superhero, She- Ra, had in 
childhood through a strong, personal voice. She states that ‘She- Ra was made for me and 
my sister ’, demonstrating the importance of diverse gender representation of role models. 
When describing that they were ‘desperate to see … women doing anything remotely 
interesting ’, Fidge uses humour to emphasise the importance of seeing role models on 
television in childhood that resonated with her desire to see ‘strong women’ being 
represented doing aspirational things. When She-Ra was taken off the air, Fidge and her 
sister protest ed by writing ‘letters to the station to express our disappointment ’. Her letter is a 
small act of protest where she exclaims, ‘Put back She- Ra. Do it… NOW!! ’ where the 
imperative voice demonstrates the impact of having a superhero role model as it gave the 
author ongoing confidence to communicate her opinion with the hope of making change. 
Question 3 
Criteria Marks 
• Explains effectively how Jansson celebrates the process of creativity , 
using appropriate supporting evidence 3 
• Explains how Jansson celebrates the process of creativity , using some 
supporting evidence 2 
• Provides some relevant information 1 
Sample answer: Jansson celebrates the process of creativity , firstly through an appreciation of physical 
materials, ‘pretty rocks’ and ‘beautiful, heavy lumber’ as the raw materials of future projects. 
Jansson lists a range of unusual stone creations: ‘mosaics, bulwarks, terraces, supports, 
smoke ovens ’, delighting in their variety ranging from decorative, useful or temporary, ‘which 
the sea will carry away ’. This ultimately shows how Jansson privileges the creative process 
over the product. By contrasting Tooti ’s creatio ns in wood, ‘the smallest salt spoon ever 
made ’ with ‘building big ’, Jansson celebrates that creativity comes in different forms and 
sizes.

Question  4    
 Criteria  Marks 
 •         Explains effectively how Ferrante portrays the ways that family 
  relationships shape identity , using well -chosen supporting evidence  4 
 •        Explains how Ferrante portrays the ways that family relationships shape 
  identity , using appropriate supporting evidence  3 
 •        Describes how Ferrante portrays the ways that family relationships 
     shape identity , with some supporting evidence  2 
 •     Provides some relevant information  1 
Sample answer: 
Ferrante portrays contrasting relationships with different parts of the narrator’s family, leading 
to shifts in her identity. Her identity is initially significantly influenced by her father ’s views, 
exemplified in the blunt and critical tone of ‘deprived me of the affection of people certainly 
without education, not smooth talkers ’, emphasising her physical separation from lower 
classes of people, including her own extended family. This belief is challenged when 
Giovanna meets her relative, Vittoria, who t eaches her the importance of ‘the heart’ which 
symbolises a person’s character as the most essential element of identity. This experience 
causes a deeper understanding of herself seen through the metaphorical shift from the formal ‘Giovanna’ to the affecti onate nickname Giannina, which signifies her new ‘relaxed 
and funny’ identity. 
Question 5 
Criteria Marks 
• Analyse s how Kooser shares insights about the experience of change, 
using well -chosen support evidence 4 
• Analyses how Kooser shares insights about the experience of change , 
using appropriate supporting evidence 3 
• Explains how Kooser shares insights about the experience of change , 
with some supporting evidence 2 
• Provides some relevant information 1 
Sample answer: 
Kooser frames the poem in letter form to signal that he wishes to share his experiences 
regarding the inevitability of change. The personification of the light to ‘walk down the hill’ 
enables him to share his delight in how the lake lights up to reveal ‘a garden/ of trees that 
grew as if by magic’. In contrast the personification of ‘winter’ in its ‘jacket’, bringing its ‘black 
horse’, depicts winter bringing darkness. He metaphorically compares summer’s light, which 
allows one to ‘keep looking out’ with win ter’s darkness which offers only a mirror that forces 
introspection. The imperative ‘must’ capture s his final insight where one ‘must now keep 
looking in’ to examine how the passage of time requires change.
