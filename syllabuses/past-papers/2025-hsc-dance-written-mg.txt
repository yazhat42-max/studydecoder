Use  of  different  aspects  of  space  to  suggest/reinforce  destiny:  
•  Performance  space –  the way in which the performance space is used 
(stage/setting/props)  
•  Personal  space –  the space which the characters occupy (negative/positive and/or  
close/far)  
•  Shape  –  the shapes that  characters create with their  body (curved/angular  and/or  
open/close and/or  complex/simple and/or  gesture and through dance technique)   
•  Levels –  the height  of  the characters in relation to the floor,  other  dancers,  prop/setting 
(high, medium and low)  
•  Direction  –  the direction that  the characters face and/or  travel   
•  Dimension  –  the size of  the movement  in relation to the character ’s  destiny  
•  Floor  pattern  –  the floor  patterns of  the characters/ensemble/other  characters  
•  Spatial  pathways  –  the direction in which the character’s body moves.  
 
Use  of  different  aspects  of  time  to  suggest/reinforce  destiny :  
•  Tempo  –  variation of  speed to reflect  the character’s destiny  
•  Duration  –  the length of  movements/phrases/sections to reflect  the character’s destiny  
•  Accent  –  distinctive  movements  that  demonstrate  the  character’s  destiny  
•  Stillness  –  the absence of  movement  to demonstrate the character ’s destiny  
•  Aural  –  the link between movement  and the music to enhance the character’s destiny.

•  Cunningham’s  distinctive  movement  vocabulary  and  techniques  emphasise  non-

•  His  belief  in  the  human  body  as  the  primary  source  of  inspiration  is  evident  in  his

•  Cunningham p  ortrays  individual  movement  expression  through  various  mediums,  
including  stage  performances,  pedestrian  movement  studies  and  unconventional 
performance spaces  
•  Examples  from h is  avant -garde works,  such as Sixteen  Dances  for  Soloist  and 
Variations  V, showcase his exploration of movement divorced from traditional storytelling

Non- narrative m ovement/r ejection of  traditional  movement:  
•  Cunningham’s  rejection  of  traditional  narrative  structures  allows  for  a  focus  on  movement  
for movement’s sake, devoid of predetermined meaning or emotional context  
•  His  use  of  chance  choreography  in  pieces  like  Story  and  Canfield  underscores his 
commitment  to randomness as a means of  generating movement  material.   
 
Choreographic  Practice:   
•  Cunningham’s  collaborative  approach  to  choreography  involves  working  closely  with  
composers,  visual  artists and designers,  enhancing the portrayal  of  individual  movement  
expression in his works  
•  Collaborations  with  artists  like  Robert  Rauschenberg  in  Points  in  Space  exemplify how  
interdisciplinary  partnerships  enrich  the  choreographic  experience  
•  Leaving a lasting impact  on the field of  modern dance.   
 
Collaboration:  
•  Cunningham’s  collaborations  with  John  Cage  and  other  artists  contribute  significantly  to  
the portrayal of individual movement expression in his choreography  
•  Works  like  Beach  Birds , which incorporates Cage’s music, exemplify the seamless  
integration  of  movement  and  sound  in  Cunningham’s  choreographic  vision.   
 
Audience  view:  
•  Radical  for  its  time,  use  of  abstract,  lacked  emotion,  alienating  and  frustrating  
•  Challenged  the  conventional  ideas  of  narrative,  musicality  and  movement  
•  Seen  as  avant -garde,  particularly with composer  John Cage  
•  Collaborations  with  composers,  visual  artists  and  other  dancers  redefined  the  relationship  
between music and dance,  allowing audiences to experience dance as an independent  
artform r  ather  than just  an accompaniment  to music  
•  Use  of  space,  time  and  movement  created  a  new language  of  dance  that  invited  viewers  
to engage with the performance in a more abstract and personal way  
•  Some  critics  viewed  his  work  as  groundbreaking,  others  saw it  as  sceptical  
•  More  positive  reception  in  Europe  and  Japan  
•  Audiences  saw its  connection  to  the  postmodern dance and minimalistic art  movement  
•  More  widely  accepted  by  younger  audiences  and  choreographers  who  drew  inspiration  
from  his work.  
 
Lucinda  Childs  
•  Childs’s  choreographic  style  is  characterised  by  complex  transitions  and  a  collaborative  
approach involving dancers and musicians  
•  Her  works  are  often  described  as  ‘conceptual  dance’,  blending  elements  of  classical  ballet  
with  innovative  movement  patterns  and  minimalist  qualities  
•  Collaborations  with  musicians  such  as  Philip  Glass  demonstrate  her  incorporation  of  
music  as  a  source  of  inspiration  and  her  ability  to  synchronise  movement  with  sound  
•  Individual movement expression through intricate choreography, collaborative endeavours  
and spatial  design,  drawing inspiration  from classical ballet to media arts.

Spatial  design and movement  patterns:  
•  Childs’s  choreography  emphasises  the  beauty  of  spatial  design,  often  plotting  each  
dancer  meticulously within the performance space  
•  Basic  locomotor  movements  like  skipping  and  turning  are  transformed  into  intricate  
choreography through repetition,  dialect  and simple geometric patterns.  
 
Influences and  background:  
•  Influenced by media artists like Jackson Pollock,  Childs integrates elements of  visual  arts 
into  her  choreography,  exploring  themes  of  space  and  form   
•  Her  classical  ballet  background  and  experience  as  an  actor  contribute  to  the  versatility  
and  depth of  her  movement  vocabulary.   
 
Performance  spaces and works:   
•  Childs’s  early  career  is  marked  by  site-specific works performed in unconventional  spaces 
such as churches,  galleries  and streets  
•  Notable  works  include  Pastime, Three  Piece, Carnation  and Street  Dance , showcasing  
her  evolution as a choreographer  and her  experimentation with different  forms and 
mediums.   
 
Legacy and contributions:  
•  Childs’s  legacy  extends  through  her  founding  of  the  Lucinda  Childs  Dance  Company  in  
1973,  which disbanded in 2018,  leaving behind a rich repertoire of  influential  works  
•  Collaborations  with  renowned  artists  like  Merce  Cunningham a  nd  her  involvement  in  
ground- breaking productions like Einstein  on  the  Beach  solidify her  status in modern 
dance.  
 
Audience  view:   
•  Incorporated minimalist aesthetics and repetitive structures, which allowed for a different 
kind of  engagement  with movement  
•  Focused  on  the  physicality  of  dance  and  the  interplay  between  movement  and  visual  
elements,  sometimes using film and  technology to alter  the perception of  the live 
performance.  This approach encouraged audiences to appreciate the subtleties of  
movement  and  the choreographic process itself, rather than just the narrative or emotional  
content  
•  Shifted  audience  perceptions from vi  ewing dance solely as a narrative or  emotional  
expression to understanding it  as a complex interplay of  movement,  space and time  
•  Lasting impact  on contemporary dance,  influencing generations of  dancers and 
choreographers  
•  Mainstream audiences  found  her  work  lacking  expression,  use  of  pedestrian  movement,  
repetition and the rejection of emotional narrative  
•  Embraced  as  an  avant -garde postmodern choreographer  
•  Appreciation  for  her  work  developed  over  time.
