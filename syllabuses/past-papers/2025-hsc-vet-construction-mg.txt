• Outlines a control measure that minimise s soil erosion 2

Sample answer: 
Control measures that can be used to minimi se soil erosion on a construction site would 
include installing a silt fence to contain run off or sediment barriers to prevent soil 
displacement , using erosion control blankets or mats to stabilise exposed areas , and 
establishing temporary vegetation or keeping vegetation to hold soil in place . Grading the site 
to reduce steep slop es and directing rain runoff can further limit erosion on a building site . 
Answers could include: 
•  Apply  mulch  to  work site  surfaces  
•  Restrict  vehicle  access  
•  Minimal  disturbance  of  natural  vegetation  and  trees  
•  Installation of silt socks around stormwater drains  
•  Establish  temporary  site  driveway  using  coarse  aggregate  
•  Vehicle  wash  bays .

Answer s could include : 
•  To  minimise  t he  risk  of  harm f  rom m anual  handling  involves  the  implementation  of  a  range  
of  techniques focused on reducing physical  strain and improving safety  
•  Tasks  need  to  be  assessed  and  planned  to  identify  risks  including  the  weight  of  the  load,  
the distance to be moved, and the physical condition of the workers  
•  Using  mechanical  aids  such  as  trolleys,  pallet  jacks  or  hoists  can  significantly  reduce  the  
need for  manual  lifting.  Workers should be trained properly in lifting techniques such as 
bending the knees,  keeping back straight  and holding load close to the bod y  
•  Team l  ifting  should  be  used  when  lifting  heavy  or  awkward  loads  
•  Ergonomic  workplace  design  such  as  adjusting  storage  heights  and  organi sing  materials  
to avoid excessive reaching or bending further minimises risks  
•  Regular  breaks  and  job  rotation  can  also  help  and  ensuring  workers  are  fit  and  healthy  for  
manual  handling tasks is essential  for  safety  
•  Provide  workers  with  proper  training  and  instruction  
•  Minimising  bending,  twisting,  overreaching  
•  Activate  core  muscles  to  brace  back  and  reduce  strain  
•  Testing  the  load  
•  PPE such  as  safety  boots,  gloves .

• Provides a comprehensive explanation of a range of strategies to 
improve resource efficiency in the construction industry 
• Provides a precise logical and cohesive response 
• Uses relevant industry terminology 
• Uses industry examples 13–15 
• Provides a thorough explanation of a range of strategies to improve 
resource efficiency in the construction industry 
• Provides a logical and cohesive response 
• Uses industry terminology 
• Uses industry examples 10–12 
• Provides a general explanation of a range of strategies to improve 
resource efficiency in the construction industry 
• Uses industry terminology and/or includes some examples 7–9 
• Shows an understanding of resource efficiency in the construction 
industry 4–6 
• Provides some relevant information 1–3 
Answer s could include : 
•  A resource  in  the  construction  industry  can  be  described  as  an  input  or  an  asset  needed  
to successfully perform the job. Resources can include but are not limited to building  
materials,  labour,  knowledge,  skills,  experience,  technology,  equipment,  tools,  time,  
money  and land.  
•  Efficiency  is  concerned  with  utilising  these  resources  in  such  a  way  as  to  extract  as  much  
benefit  and value from t  hem as  possible while minimising waste.  Waste is more than 
offcuts,  rubbish and byproducts of  the building process ,  it  can  also  include  time,  money  
skills and space.  For  example,  getting a skilled tradesperson to pick up a materials order  
could be considered a waste of  expertise if  a labourer  or  apprentice were available to 
perform t  he task .  
•  Depending  on  the  goals  and  the  objectives  of  the  building  project  or  the  PCBU,  resource  
efficiency can essentially reduce environmental  impact,  improve safety and working 
conditions,  minimise production costs and maximise productivity .  
•  Resource  efficiency  can  be  influenced  by  the  design  of  a  building  or  structure.  For  
instance,  giving  appropriate  thought  to  the  availability,  size  and  characteristics  of  
proposed materials can result  in waste reduction and cost  savings.  Designing spaces and 
components to suit  standard material  sizes can reduce the need for  cutting and joining.  
For  example,  plasterboard  makes  up  a  large  percentage  of  building  waste  and  can  be  
difficult  to recycle.  
•  Designing wall  and ceiling heights to match full  sheet  widths would limit  waste,  speed up 
installation  and  decrease  clean  up  time,  resulting  in  less  labour  and  waste  disposal costs,  
not  to mention the environmental  benefit .  
•  Sequencing  material  deliveries  so  that  they  arrive  just  as  they  are  needed  can  also  
improve  resource  efficiency.  Having  materials  stockpiled  or  stored  on  site  takes  up  
valuable space that  can impede the access of  workers and equipment.  Navigating and

working  around  these  obstacles  can  decrease  productivity  and  increase  the  likelihood  of  
damage,  resulting in unnecessary waste and financial  consequences .  
•  The  correct  sequencing  of  trades  and  rostering  of  staff  is  also  crucial  to  achieving  
resource efficiency. A lot of time can be wasted if trades can ’t freely access the site to  
perform t  heir  work or  if  preceding trades have not  completed their  stage of  the project.  
Making  sure  that  the  correct  number  of  workers  are  available  is  vital  for  an  efficiently  run  
site.  Too few  workers, and the job can become unnecessarily arduous, unsafe and  
time-consuming,  too many and the site can become congested,  also posing sa fety  
concerns,  potential  for  conflict  and workers being under -utilised.  The use of  Gantt  charts 
and construction schedules can help to manage labour  resources .

regulations) .  
•  Maintaining  tools  and  equipment  to  ensure  correct  operation .
