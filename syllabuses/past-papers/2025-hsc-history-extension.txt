 Question 1 (25 marks) 
Evaluate Bongiorno’s interpretation of the aims and purposes of history. Use Source A and at least TWO other sources to support your argument. 
Source A 
To study the history of your own place – your own nation, if you like – has particular
attractions as well as dangers that are fairly well known. There is the allure ofexploring your own sense of group belonging, your own physical and culturalenvironment, the stories of your own tribe . . . But we also know that, in the context of asymmetries* of power, the practice of history has imposed its own oppressions.It has often been a handmaiden** to the powerful . . . History’s claims have been used by the powerful to mobilise the most vicious of impulses. Its assumptions,methodologies and temporalities*** have bolstered colonialism, rationalised warand genocide, and justified environmental destruction. I have sometimes foundcause to remind my own students when they begin to wonder whether studyinghistory is really all that important, that people kill one another over the meaning ofhistory. It can be a deadly business. 
It should therefore be treated as a matter of deadly seriousness; we need to educate
students of history and train historians with the same level of rigour that the worldexpects of those who train doctors to look after our bodies, engineers to build ourbridges, accountants to attend to our finances and lawyers to keep us out of prison.We – and our students – have a critical civic role to perform in what has been calledthe post-truth era, with its indifference to veracity****, contempt for expertise, andreplacement of history with myth in the interests of leader, class, faction, party ornation . . . 
. . . I do want to make a case here for the public life of history, whose health seems 
to be a prerequisite for turning any of this around. This does not mean that we allgo out and become ‘princes of print’ or ‘queens of the screen’ . . . But it does mean that we need to take much more seriously how we can contribute to the vitality ofthe public sphere in our democracy, in a world where evidence-based knowledgeand civil debate are under intense pressure . . . we need to be willing to ask ourselves hard questions about [history’s] public purpose . . . 
F
RANK  BONGIORNO
What is history for? Australian Historical Association Presidential Address, 2024
