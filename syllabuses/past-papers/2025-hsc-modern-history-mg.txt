Question  10  (a)  
Answer s could i  nclude :  
The  various  economic  policies  of  the  Soviet  Union  throughout  the period 1917  to  1941 did 
not  all  align with Bolshevik ideological  principles.  At  times,  economic policy required 
ideological compromise  for the sake of maintaining  power.  Stalin ’s economic policies,  while 
addressing some elements of  Bolshevik  ideology  such  as  collective  ownership  and  a  planned  
economy,  also made significant  reinterpretation of  Marxist- Leninist  principles.  
Key  points  could i  nclude:  
•  The  New Economic Po  licy:  allowed  a  return  of  capitalist  elements  in  the  economy,  
encouraging peasants’  opportunity to increase their  wealth and small- scale trade within 
the cities. The NEP abandoned the restrictive  measure s of  war  communism,  which  had 
introduced  a command economy and lost  considerable suppor  t  from am ong the workers 
and peasants.  
•  Debate  over  moderni sation: Competing visions for the USSR, given Russia ’s lack of  
capitalist  development,  required major  ideological  revision leading Bolshevik figures.  
Some  such  as  Bukharin  saw the  NEP as  a  long-term vehicle to build  capitalism  in  Russia,  
before a truly communist  society could be established.  Others on the Left,  such as Trotsky 
believed the NEP w  as a temporary  measure  to  restore  the  economy  which  had  been  
devastated  after  the Civil  War.  Once Stalin rose to power,  he set  about  ending the NE P,  
arguing that  it  had created new cl  ass enemies which contradicted the notion of  a classless 
society.  
•  Forced  collectivi sation: While Stalin could claim that the policy of collectivi sation  fulfilled  
the ideological principle of shared ownership, the methods by which this was implemented  
against  a reluctant  peasantry betrayed Lenin’ s concept  of  a smychka,  or  alliance between 
peasant  and worker.  Furthermore, the  prioriti sation  of  grain exports to raise capital  led to 
widespread  famine,  abandoning  the  Bolshevik  principle  of  ‘from each according to their 
ability’  to  ‘each according to their  need ’.  
•  Five- year  plans:  The greatest  betrayal  of  Bolshevism w  as the repression of  the industrial  
working  class  under  the  five-year  plans.  All  remains of  worker  solidarity,  workplace  rights, 
and collective decision -making  were  abandoned  for  the  sake  of  production  targets.

Question  10  (b)  
Answer s could i  nclude :  
The  growth  of  the  Communist  Party  was  key  to  Stalin’s  political  transformation,  enabling  him  
to purge rivals and enforce the Great Terror. However, propaganda and censorship also  
played a crucial  role in solidifying his dictatorship.  
Key  points  could i  nclude:  
•  Stalin  as  General  Secretary:  Controlled  membership,  enrolling  approximately  500,000  
new m embers by 1925.  These new m  embers were loyal  to Stalin,  helping to expand his 
bureaucratic control.  
•  Expansion  of  the  party  and  Stalin’s  control:  rapid  party  growth  allowed  Stalin  to  place  
loyalists  in  key  positions  securing  obedience  and  stifling  opposition.  
•  Purges  and  elimination  of  rivals:  As  the  Party reached mass proportions it  became more 
difficult  to control.  To eliminate potential  dissent,  Stalin was able to justify purges on the 
basis that  the party had been infiltrated by counter  revolutionaries.  This led to mass 
arrests,  executions and suppression of  opposition.  
•  Show trials  and  terror:  public  trials  of  former  Bolsheviks  fabricated  a  Trotskyite  conspiracy  
further justifying purges. The Great Terror extended to military leaders, intellectuals, and  
citizens,  fostering a culture of  fear  and obedience.  
•  Propaganda  and  censorship:  Stalin’s  cult  of  personality  was  re-enforced through 
state -controlled media,  art,  and education,  portraying him as  the ‘Great  Leader’  while 
suppressing alternative views and historical  truths.

Question  13  (b)  
Answer s could i  nclude :  
The  Second  Indochina  War  had  a  devastating  impact  on  civilians  in  both  North  and  South  
Vietnam,  as  it  led  to  widespread  displacement,  loss  of  life,  and  severe  economic  disruption.  
In North Vietnam, the war intensified the government ’s control  over  the population through 
forced mobilisation and the destruction of infrastructure, while in South Vietnam, civilians  
suffered from i  ntense military operations,  bombings,  and the threat  of  both communist  and 
anti-communist  forces.  
Key  points  could include:  
•  Civilian  Casualties  and  Loss  of  Life:  Death  tolls  as  a  result  of  the  direct  and  indirect  
consequences of  bombing campaigns (such as Operation Rolling Thunder),  ground 
operations,  and guerrilla warfare on civilian populations.  
•  The  impact  of  chemical  warfare,  such  as  the  use  of  Agent  Orange  and  other  chemical  
defoliants,  leading to health issues and birth defects among civilians in both North and 
South  Vietnam.  
•  Mass  executions  and  atrocities  and  the  treatment  of  civilians  in  contested  areas,  including  
reprisals and massacres by both sides of the conflict, My Lai Massacre.  
•  Displacement  and  Refugees:  Internal displacement due to military operations and  
bombings forced civilians to flee their  homes,  particularly in rural  areas of  South Vietnam.  
•  Impact of the  ‘strategic hamlet ’  program and  the forced relocation of  South Vietnamese 
civilians into fortified villages by the US and  South Vietnamese government,  and its effect  
on the civilian population.  
•  Economic  Disruption:  The  bombing  of  roads,  bridges,  villages,  and  industrial  areas,  and  
its  impact  on  the  economy  and  daily  life  for  civilians  in  both  North  and  South  Vietnam.  
•  Repression  and  Control  by  the  Government:  North  Vietnamese  government  policies  such  
as the implementation of  strict  policies in the North,  including forced labour,  mobilisation 
for war efforts, and the suppression of dissent.  
•  The  tactics  used  by  the  South  Vietnamese  government  and  the  US military  to  maintain  
control,  including arrests,  curfews,  and violent  reprisals against  suspected communist  
sympathis ers.
