Teaching advice for Stage 6
Teaching advice for Geographical inquiry skills (Year 11)
Content
* Geographical inquiry skills
Teaching advice
The process of geographical inquiry involves individual or collaborative investigations that start with geographical questions and proceed through the collection, evaluation, interpretation and analysis of information to the development of conclusions and proposals for actions.
The stages of a complete inquiry in Stage 6 include:
Acquiring geographical information
Students develop geographical questions and acquire (either independently or with teacher guidance) primary data and secondary information.
Processing geographical information
Students represent, interpret, synthesise and analyse data and information. They should also evaluate data and information for reliability, bias, validity and usefulness.
Applying geographical understanding
Students apply the information they have processed to evaluate options, propose actions and predict outcomes. They apply their knowledge and understanding to other geographic contexts.
Communicating geographical understanding
Students communicate geographical understanding, findings, conclusions and/or ideas for a range of audiences and reflect on the process and effectiveness of geographical inquiry. Students should be encouraged to use different modes of communication and a range of tools to represent and communicate data and information.
Teaching advice
During an inquiry process, students should apply their geographical skills and use geographical tools, including digital and spatial technologies. Inquiries may vary in their scope and geographical context.
Fieldwork provides opportunities for students to be involved in an active inquiry outside the classroom. This may occur in the school, offsite or in some cases virtually. Accessibility should be a key consideration when selecting sites for fieldwork. There is additional advice on fieldwork in the Teaching and learning support section.
While the Geographical Investigation offers students the opportunity to apply a broad range of geographical inquiry skills, it is not intended that students would always undertake a complete inquiry process for all focus areas in Year 11 and Year 12. Teachers can provide students with data to represent or analyse rather than have students acquire or collect the information themselves.
When planning and programming it may be helpful to devise a mapping grid to ensure geographical inquiry skills are distributed across focus areas in Stage 6.
When undertaking geographical inquiry, students must adhere to ethical practices, including:
* respecting confidentiality and anonymity
* avoiding use of deception or misleading information
* preventing physical and/or emotional risk or harm
* applying protocols for community consultation and Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP)
* obtaining permission to access private property or restricted areas
* minimising damage to the natural environment
* observing referencing conventions to avoid plagiarism
* storing data appropriately, eg securely, depersonalising data collected
* attributing sources, eg information, data, visual materials.
Some students with disability may require adjustments and/or additional support to engage in the geographical inquiry process.

Teaching advice for Geographical tools (Year 11)
Content
* Geographical tools
Teaching advice
Geographical tools are used during an inquiry to acquire, process, apply and communicate geographical information. Geographical tools enable students to synthesise the findings of a geographical inquiry. There are 5 categories of geographical tools:
* maps
* fieldwork
* graphs and statistics
* spatial technologies
* visual representations.
Maps
Maps include digital and non-digital mediums, and take many forms, including:
* large-scale and small-scale maps
* relief maps
* choropleth maps
* flowline maps
* isoline maps (such as synoptic charts or rainfall maps)
* land use maps
* physical maps
* political maps
* cultural mapping (spatial representation of Aboriginal perspectives about stories, relationships and/or practices)
* précis maps (summary maps showing the main features of an area)
* cadastral maps (showing land ownership boundaries)
* thematic maps (such as geology or population distribution maps)
* tactile maps
* topographic maps
* special-purpose maps (such as digital elevation models or bike trail maps).
Maps are used to locate, visualise, represent, display and record spatial data.
Fieldwork
Fieldwork is an integral and mandatory part of the study of Geography. It enables an understanding of geographical processes and geographical inquiry. Fieldwork should be an enjoyable experience that enhances learning opportunities for all students because it provides for a variety of teaching and learning approaches.
The experience of active engagement in fieldwork helps to create and nurture a lifelong interest in, and enthusiasm for the world in which students live. There is further information about fieldwork in the Teaching and learning support section.
Graphs and statistics
Graphs - also called charts - take many forms and include digital and non-digital modes, such as:
* tally charts
* pictographs
* column graphs
* line graphs
* pie/sector graphs
* ternary graphs
* weather maps
* climate graphs
* population profiles.
Statistics also take many forms and include digital and non-digital modes.
Graphs and statistics are used to collate, organise, illustrate, summarise, compare and predict patterns, relationships and trends in geographical data and information.
There are several ways to explicitly teach students to analyse the geographical data captured in graphs and statistics. Two common methods are PQE (pattern, quantify, exceptions) and Layers of inference which provide a framework to analyse and interpret geographic tools.
Spatial technologies
Spatial technologies include any software or hardware that interacts with real-world locations, such as:
* virtual maps
* satellite images
* global positioning systems (GPS)
* geographic information systems (GIS)
* remote sensing
* augmented reality (overlaying digital information on a map or in the physical world).
Spatial technologies are used to collect, record, represent, manipulate, analyse and communicate spatial data.
Visual representations
Visual representations take many forms and include digital and non-digital mediums, such as:
* diagrams
* images
* photographs
* paintings
* illustrations
* symbols
* tactile models
* posters
* collages
* cartoons
* multimedia
* infographics
* mind maps.
Visual representations are used to display, visualise, analyse and communicate geographical data and information. These representations can be used by students to communicate geographic information in a variety of ways.
Teaching advice
When planning and programming it may be helpful to devise a mapping grid to ensure the use of geographical tools is distributed across focus areas in Stage 6. Teachers can make decisions about the appropriate tools to support learning in each focus area, taking into consideration students' needs, interests and abilities. Accessibility should be a key consideration when selecting geographical tools. For example, selecting images with alternative text descriptions, infographics with transcripts, or graphs and charts with appropriate colour contrast.
It is important for teachers to unpack examples of these geographical tools for students and explicitly teach students the various ways they can be used to support geographical inquiry.
Some students with disability may require adjustments to access geographical tools, eg tactile maps to investigate landforms and features, colour-blind friendly maps, assistive technology such as a screen reader to access geographical information, video/audio recording equipment to capture soundscapes, augmentative and alternative communication to communicate geographical information and using accessible locations for fieldwork.

Teaching advice for Earth's natural systems
Content
* Earth's natural systems
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate the diverse landscapes of the Earth's surface and its distinctive physical features to inspire curiosity and wonder. They examine the cycles, circulations, interconnections and spatial patterns that combine to form the Earth's integrated systems, and investigate natural processes, cycles and circulations that change the Earth's land and water cover. It is a study of the Earth's interdependent systems, the complexities and connectedness of different components.
This focus area provides opportunities for students to develop a holistic understanding of the natural world. The geographical understanding developed though this focus area can provide a foundation for Stage 6.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
This section of the course focuses on processes and systems, not a particular ecosystem. Students could investigate natural cycles and circulations across a variety of global environments and contexts. Alternatively, they may learn about the concept of natural cycles and flows through the study of a particular place such as a mountain environment, or through a natural phenomenon/event such as a cyclone.
Geographical inquiry skills and tools
The geographical inquiry skills and tools content are to be integrated as appropriate.
A broad range of geographical tools should be applied to this topic in various ways. The following examples illustrate some approaches:
* using spatial technologies to investigate and map physical features across locations
* identifying, collecting and recording primary data using fieldwork methods in the investigation of natural systems
* constructing a transect to describe spatial change and associations in a specific area
* recognising the key features of synoptic charts and climate graphs to describe atmospheric processes
* orientating a photograph of a local environment to a map.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of the Earth's natural systems to the contemporary world, including to particular professions and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* meteorological forecasting
* planning hazard mitigation
* local council maintenance of coastal areas
* practising environmental law.
These examples provide opportunities to link geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment, such as:
* meteorologist
* emergency management planning officer
* environmental engineer
* marine biologist
* land and environmental protection solicitor.
Examples of fieldwork opportunities
Examples of fieldwork could include investigating local systems and their associated natural cycles, such as:
* coasts 
* wetlands (freshwater or intertidal)
* flood plains.
Fieldwork techniques can be used to collect biophysical data (within school grounds or offsite) such as recording and observing weather data or modelling catchment runoff patterns.
Glossary terms and concepts
The syllabus glossary includes information to assist with understanding terms and concepts used in this focus area, such as climate, continentality, geomorphic, geomorphic processes, inspirational landscapes, Overview Effect.

Teaching advice for People, patterns and processes
Content
* People, patterns and processes
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate evidence of human diversity across the Earth's surface. They examine the spatial patterns and extent of the human footprint, and the human transformations shaping those patterns. Students investigate the unique character of places and how various human processes are shaping them.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
Geographical inquiry skills and tools
The geographical inquiry skills and tools are to be integrated as appropriate and content can be explored using geographical tools. The following examples illustrate some approaches:
* calculating density of human features in an area using a map
* examining tangible and intangible cultural features using a variety of differing types of maps
* constructing a cultural map that represents human diversity across a nation or continent
* constructing a national or continental transect, identifying evidence of human diversity, including land use and social or cultural characteristics
* interpreting population profiles of different places to understand trends, change and future implications
* representing the movement of people or resources using a diagram or map
* constructing a précis map to show land use
* calculating areas of land use as a percentage or ratio using maps, graphs or statistics
* using a survey or interview to collect primary data
* applying ethical research practices.
This focus area provides opportunities to incorporate a variety of geographical tools to develop visual literacy such as using geographic information system (GIS) to investigate change over time.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of people, patterns and processes to the contemporary world, including to particular professions and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* advising diplomats and politicians
* practising investigative journalism
* participating in non-government organisations (NGOs)
* providing background information for tourist agencies.
These examples provide opportunities to link geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment such as:
* demographer
* support worker - refugee services
* tourism operator
* diplomatic attaché.
Examples of fieldwork opportunities
Study 1: Human resilience in diverse environments
Investigating a local environment using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as: 
* observing spatial patterns and characteristics
* mapping natural resources, including creative use of resources
* using GIS and analysing data to evaluate impacts and responses to change.
Study 2: Local places and global economic change
Investigating changes to local places using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as:
* photographing types of infrastructure investment
* researching changes to employment patterns through interviews or surveys
* mapping land use and ownership
* using spatial technologies such as drones to investigate urban growth.
Study 3: Place and cultural change
Investigating local places and/or cultures using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as:
* observing and recording examples of culture of place - for example, noise, colour, architecture
* interviewing local Aboriginal Elders about their experiences of cultural maintenance and change
* surveying local residents on perceptions of cultural change.
Read about Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander principles and protocols
Study 4: Political power and contested spaces
Investigating political power and contested spaces using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as:
* virtual fieldwork - using spatial technologies to investigate spatial patterns and characteristics of a contested space
* a local contested space such as a cultural heritage site with development prospects.
Study 5: Technological advances and the transformation of places
Investigating places using geographical inquiry skills and tools focusing on aspects such as: 
* visiting a port or airport to observe and collect data on the impact of technological advances and sustainable practices
* creating field sketches or sketch maps to show transport links within an urban centre
* using plans such as transport plans to consider or predict what transformation could look like and the potential impacts at a site.
Glossary terms and concepts
The syllabus glossary includes information to assist with understanding terms and concepts used in this focus area, such as culture, economic integration, geopolitical, human wellbeing, regional centres, settlement pattern, sociocultural, transformation.

Teaching advice for Human-environment interactions
Content
* Human-environment interactions
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate the global nature of land cover change from a temporal and spatial perspective, as they examine the long-term development of natural systems, compared to the short time frame of human activity. They investigate evidence for, and causes of, climate change, as well as the role of humans in contributing to land cover change. Students investigate the interaction between the Earth's natural systems and people.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
When choosing one of the three study options for this focus area, consider areas that support the concepts of local, regional, national and global scale.
Study 1: A geographic region
A region is a place that has a commonly recognised identity (although there are varying perspectives in terms of boundaries). Regions can be studied at a variety of scales, for example:  
* Liverpool Plains
* Murray-Darling Basin
* the Caribbean
* the Arctic.
Regional boundaries can be fluid and this can depend on perspective. There is likely to be a zone of transition. The selection of a study should consider what makes the region different to surrounding areas.
Study 2: A contemporary hazard
The selected hazard should be considered in general and then a case study approach can be used to investigate the management and responses to one specific event. A contemporary hazard should be viewed as one that has occurred this century.  
Study 3: Climate change
This is a more in-depth study of climate change than the introductory content in the Overview of change to Earth's natural systems over time section of this focus area. Scale is an important concept in this study option - for example, investigating impacts, challenges and responses at a range of scales. Teachers should consider building on the concepts and selecting different examples to those that may be studied in Stages 4-5.  
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of human-environment interactions to the contemporary world, including particular professions and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* community development
* land development
* tourism management
* hazard assessment
* planning hazard mitigation
* natural resource management
* international cooperation in response to melting glaciers and retreating ice sheets.
This provides opportunities to link the geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment such as:
* mine geologist
* hydrological engineer  
* outdoor education trainer
* climate change analyst
* bushfire management advisor.
Geographical inquiry skills and tools
The geographical inquiry skills and tools content are to be integrated as appropriate.
The following examples illustrate some approaches:
* constructing, interpreting and analysing graphs and statistics to describe human-environment interactions
* constructing mind maps or diagrams to illustrate the impact of natural and human change
* using photographs to identify spatial associations, interactions and change
* identifying the location of a place on small and large-scale maps
* developing geographical questions to investigate challenges and opportunities of human-environment interactions.
Examples of fieldwork opportunities
Study 1: A geographic region
Investigating a region using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as: 
* local fieldwork - interview residents to gain perspectives about the character of their region or regional boundaries 
* virtual fieldwork (international study) - using spatial technologies to investigate unique characteristics and evidence of change.
Study 2: A contemporary hazard
Investigating a natural or ecological hazard (in general), then conducting fieldwork to support the management or impacts of one event, using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as: 
* photographing evidence of one natural or ecological hazard - for example, flood level signs, burnt vegetation, social distancing signs, QR codes
* constructing a transect representing an area of forest to measure new growth after a fire
* drawing précis maps of flood zones to compare with older flood zone maps.
Study 3: Climate change
Investigating local environments using geographical inquiry skills focusing on aspects such as: 
* engaging with climate change experts via social media or virtual meeting platforms to assess varying perspectives
* measuring surface temperature at locations around a student's local environment to explain concepts (such as albedo or aspect) that link to climate change
* observing plant communities and predicting change linked to increasing temperatures, reduced precipitation or greater intensity of extreme weather events.
Key glossary terms and concepts
The syllabus glossary includes information to assist with understanding terms and concepts used in this focus area, such as climate change, ecological hazard, geographic region, land cover, natural hazard, risk management.

Teaching advice for Geographical investigation
Content
* Geographical investigation
Teaching advice
Students plan and conduct ONE Geographical Investigation to develop their understanding of the nature of geographical inquiry through practical research and the application of geographical concepts, skills and tools.
The Geographical Investigation provides opportunities for all students to apply geographical concepts, skills and tools through practical research in:
* their local area, or
* an area that is accessible for the purpose of primary data collection.
The Geographical Investigation should use active inquiry methodologies to extend an area of individual or group interest that relates to the Year 11 course. It provides opportunity for individual and/or collaborative inquiry. The hours spent on the Geographical Investigation are in addition to the 12 hours of mandatory fieldwork required in Year 11.
Teaching advice
There are explicit requirements for undertaking the Geographical Investigation. Each step in the syllabus content for this focus area is an essential part of the process.
The Geographical Investigation topic/inquiry should be:
* narrowly focused but with enough scope to apply geographical inquiry skills and tools
* situated in a convenient location.
This will enable students to plan, sequence and answer their geographical inquiry questions through the collection of primary data and secondary information that they have sourced themselves.
Teachers should model the steps in a geographical inquiry process (and how to use a variety of geographical tools) to support students in applying their knowledge and skills to successfully complete the Geographical Investigation.
For additional information refer to the Geographical skills and tools Teaching advice
Students are encouraged to be present and communicate their findings using methods that suit their context, skills, preferences and individual circumstances.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an understanding of the relevance and the nature of geographical inquiry and how it can contribute to effective management of places and environments. This also allows students to engage with geographical opportunities and challenges in the public sphere, including the role and influence of:
* research institutions
* government
* advocacy groups
* the media
* the individual.
This provides an opportunity to link the geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment such as:
* research scientist
* politician   
* cartographer
* town planner
* environmental lobbyist
* environmental journalist or filmmaker.
Key glossary terms and concepts
The syllabus glossary includes information to assist with understanding terms and concepts used in this focus area, such as cartographic conventions, copyright, ethical practices, Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP), intellectual property (IP), primary data, secondary information, spatial change.

Teaching advice for Global sustainability
Content
* Global sustainability
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate the geographic concept of sustainability, with a focus on the global context. They develop an understanding of the role of sustainability in meeting the needs of present and future generations around the world and maintaining the ecological processes on which all life depends. They examine principles of, and actions for, sustainability, including practices of Indigenous Peoples.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
The focus is on an economic activity and although visiting a local enterprise, representative of the global activity can be a relevant aspect for fieldwork, the global context and nature of sustainability strategies for the economic activity should be at the forefront when planning and programming this focus area.
Teachers selecting a global economic activity should consider the need to examine a range of strategies implemented at a global scale, evaluate sustainability using one or more criteria and critically analyse one strategy. In this context, the critical analysis of one strategy would include evaluation.
There are several evaluation criteria that can be used, such as the Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) indicators and industry certification standards. Teachers may like to use some examples of sustainability criteria in the sample unit of work; however, this list is not comprehensive or prescriptive. Due to the contemporary nature of this focus area, new information on and examples of sustainability are constantly evolving.
Global sustainability is a complex topic that underpins learning in other areas. A deep exploration of the essential concepts is required. There are various understandings of the difference between sustainability and sustainable development, that help students develop an understanding of the field of sustainability. In the Geography Stage 6 syllabus, the pillars of sustainability are social, economic, environmental and cultural.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of human activities and sustainability to the contemporary world, including to particular areas of employment and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* developing criteria to measure and evaluate sustainability
* advising diplomats and politicians
* managing or advising organisations and businesses
* undertaking sustainability assessments.

Teaching advice for Rural and urban places
Content
* Rural and urban places
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate the nature of rural and urban settlements and the global pattern of urban change. They examine the factors causing change in rural and urban locations.
Students study a rural place, an urban place and a large city, to develop an understanding of the geographical processes influencing their character, and responses and strategies for enhancing sustainability and the quality of life.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
It is recommended the rural place and the place within a larger urban settlement chosen as a study are conveniently located to facilitate access for fieldwork.  
If selecting a regional centre as the rural place it is ideal that its location has a rural context. For example, Newcastle and Wollongong are not generally considered to be rural regional centres.
There is flexibility for teachers when selecting a large city of more than 5 million people, outside Australia. However, information on the city selected must be able to provide sufficient depth on each of the aspects listed in the syllabus content in terms of geographical processes, challenges, responses and opportunities for enhancing sustainability.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of rural and urban places to the contemporary world, including particular areas of employment and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* urban and regional planning
* designing effective city infrastructure
* planning the delivery of community services
* monitoring environmental quality
* evaluating sustainability
* preserving heritage sites.
This provides an opportunity to link geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment such as:
* town planner
* structural engineer
* rural health provider
* rural development officer (local government) 
* environmental advisor (state government).
Geographical inquiry skills and tools
The geographical inquiry skills and tools content are to be integrated as appropriate.
A broad range of geographical tools should be applied to this focus area, in various ways, including:
* using GIS, satellite and aerial photographs to describe the rate of change in rural and urban settlements
* interpreting a range of sources such as choropleth maps and transects to describe urban change
* interpreting trends from graphs and data about the growth, decline or changes within different rural and urban settlements
* calculating the time of day when a photograph was taken and relating a photograph to a map of a streetscape.
Opportunities for fieldwork
These may include:
* using primary fieldwork methods and geographical tools to research the character and changes to urban and/or rural places such as surveys for liveability and quality of environment
* synthesising and evaluating fieldwork data about change in a country town or suburb
* shade mapping and land-use mapping using drones
* surveying local residents about housing affordability or service provision.

Teaching advice for Ecosystems and global biodiversity
Content
* Ecosystems and global biodiversity
Teaching advice
Content focus
Students investigate the functioning of ecosystems and trends in global biodiversity. They examine the value of ecosystems and biodiversity, the global state of ecosystems, and the role of Indigenous Peoples in contemporary management practices.
The content is described with examples; however, the outcomes must guide the selection of studies, fieldwork and assessment.
It is important to revisit the requirements for this focus area in the Course Overview when programming.
Teaching advice
Consider selecting ecosystems with readily available contemporary data on biodiversity and that link closely to the first section of the focus area - Ecosystems and biodiversity.
The role of contemporary research and innovation in the sustainable management of ecosystems is crucial to the understanding of this topic. Some examples may include:
* the COTsBOT (Crown-of-Thorns starfish robot) in coral reef ecosystems
* real-time deforestation monitoring across the Amazon rainforest using drones equipped with remote sensing technology
* the work of national and international research organisations and institutes, including IGOs and NGOs - for example, Forest Research UK and Earthwatch and the CSIRO.
There are a range of concepts in this focus area, such as tipping points, shifting baselines and feedback loops. It is important for teachers to unpack these concepts and apply them as and where appropriate.
Applying geography in the contemporary world
Students develop an appreciation of the relevance of geographical understanding of ecosystems and global biodiversity to the contemporary world, including particular areas of employment and responsible management.
Examples of the application of geographical understanding and methods could include:
* conservation and sustainable management by Indigenous Peoples
* managing a national park
* ecological mapping for surveyors
* evaluating dune-stabilisation programs.
These examples provide opportunities to link geographical inquiry skills and tools to particular areas of employment such as:
* ecologist
* park ranger or guide
* Landcare coordinator
* coastal management engineer
* environmental compliance coordinator
* education officer
* zoologist.
Geographical inquiry skills and tools
The geographical inquiry skills and tools content are to be integrated as appropriate.
A broad range of geographical tools should be applied to this focus in various ways, including:
* calculating the gradient of a slope and the vertical exaggeration of a cross-section for a specific ecosystem
* interpreting synoptic charts to understand predominant weather characteristics of different ecosystems
* constructing a précis map from an aerial photograph or satellite image to describe the spatial pattern of an ecosystem
* using flowcharts of food webs and nutrient cycles to examine ecosystem functioning
* using graphs and statistics to analyse trends in the state of biodiversity.
Opportunities for fieldwork
This may include:
* using fieldwork methods and geographical tools to investigate ecosystem functioning
* using fieldwork evidence to assess the effectiveness of ecosystem management practices
* virtual fieldwork for an overseas case study (ensuring students form geographical questions that they can answer through data collection, as opposed to a research assignment).

Glossary
Geography 11-12 (2022) Terms
benefit sharing
Formal and mutually agreed terms for the ongoing, equitable distribution of benefits, arising from the application or commercial utilisation of knowledge, practices and/or resources. Benefit sharing agreements with Indigenous Peoples may relate to Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP), such as knowledges and practices associated with sustainable management of land and resources.
biocapacity
The capacity of nature/ecosystems to produce and renew the resources that people use and to absorb and filter the waste generated by human activities, within a limited period of time.
biodiversity
The variety of living organisms within a particular habitat, ecosystem, biome, or globally.
biophysical
Biological and physical elements of the environment. It includes various life forms, both plant and animal, and elements such as temperature, light, humidity and soil nutrients.
cartographic conventions
Accepted practices associated with constructing and interpreting maps, e.g. using a border, orientation or compass point, legend or key, title, scale, giving latitude readings before longitude.
climate
The average types of weather, including seasonal variations, experienced by a place or region over a long period of time.
climate change
A long-term change in regional or global climate patterns, e.g. annual precipitation, frequency of weather events.
continentality
The effect of land mass on climatic conditions/patterns.
demographic transition
A model consisting of various stages used to show population change over time, including the influence of birth rates and death rates.
diversity
In a geographic context, diversity refers to variations within and between places, environments, natural or human phenomena, e.g. in relation to biophysical characteristics, culture, religion, settlement, economic activity.
dynamic equilibrium
In the context of Geography 11-12, when an ecosystem is able to maintain its natural balance and remain relatively stable, subject to gradual changes through natural succession. An ecosystem that is in a state of dynamic equilibrium is able to return to its balanced state in response to natural and/or human stresses.
ecological disturbance
Temporary changes or events in an ecosystem that cause disturbance to its functioning, e.g. increased mortality of organisms, changes in spatial patterning. Ecosystems are typically resilient to ecological disturbance.
ecological hazard
A biological hazard that has the potential to impact adversely on the wellbeing of people or the environment more generally. Examples of ecological hazards include malaria, coronavirus, Dengue fever, rabies, cholera, locust plagues, invasive species.
ecological integrity
The ability of an ecosystem to support and naturally maintain ecological processes, species, a diverse community of organisms, and other important characteristics, with minimal or no intervention through human management.
economic integration
The process by which places become increasingly linked and interdependent economically. This involves the reduction or elimination of the barriers to the flow of goods, services and factors of production between places and nations.
ecosystem
A system formed by the interaction of biotic organisms with each other and with the abiotic components of the environment in which they live.
While an ecosystem may be synonymous with a specific environment, the 'system' approach enables a focus on the various components and their interconnected nature.
environment
The living and non-living elements of the Earth's surface and atmosphere. Where unqualified, it includes human changes to the Earth's surface, e.g. croplands, planted forests, buildings and roads.
ethical practices
The application of fundamental ethical principles when undertaking research and collecting information, e.g. confidentiality, informed consent, citation and integrity of data. This includes, but is not limited to:
* respecting confidentiality and anonymity
* avoiding use of deception or misleading information
* preventing physical and/or emotional risk or harm
* obtaining permission to access private property or restricted areas
* minimising damage to the natural environment
* observing referencing conventions to avoid plagiarism
* storing data appropriately, e.g. securely, depersonalising data collected
* acknowledging the provenance of sources, e.g. information, data, visual materials.
feedback loops
Feedback loops are reactions in response to environmental change. Positive feedback loops cause one or more components to increase overall, creating a negative impact on the ecosystem. A negative feedback loop has a positive impact on the ecosystem because it decreases the impact of change, bringing it closer to dynamic equilibrium.
geographic region
A region exhibits shared natural or human characteristics, e.g. political, economic, social, cultural, climate, land/water cover, vegetation, that distinguishes the region from neighbouring regions. Regions can be divisions of a nation, or larger than a nation.
geographical methods
The tools and skills associated with the discipline of geography that geographers use to develop their understanding of places, environments, and geographical phenomena.
geographical processes
The physical and human processes that form and transform the world, e.g. the water cycle, erosion, migration and urbanisation. Geographical processes can work in combination and operate within and between places.
geomorphic
Relating to the form of the landscape and other features on the Earth's surface.
geomorphic processes
Natural processes that transform the lithosphere to create distinctive landscapes and landforms e.g. tectonic activity, weathering, erosion and deposition.
geopolitical
Politics and international relations as influenced by geographical factors, e.g. related to space, place and environment.
human diversity
Variations in how people use the places and spaces they occupy, including the nature of their settlements, economies, cultures and societies.
human wellbeing
The quality of life of a population, e.g. access to opportunities, healthcare, education, infrastructure, a clean environment.
informal settlements
Areas where housing or shelter is constructed on land where the occupants either have no legal claim, occupy illegally and/or are not in compliance with planning and building regulations. These settlements typically lack provision of amenities and services.
inspirational landscape
Landscapes that stimulate emotional responses based on aesthetic elements, e.g. their dramatic physical form, vibrant colour, arrangement of physical features, diversity of plant species. An inspirational landscape may be experienced directly or through soundscapes and tactile representations, e.g. physical models.
land cover
The natural and artificial features and structures that cover the land's surface, e.g. trees, grass, crops, wetlands, water, ice, buildings and pavement.
liveability
An assessment of the quality of a place. The concept of liveability has been linked to a range of factors, e.g. health, sense of safety, access to services, cost of living, comfortable living standards, mobility and transport, air quality and social participation.
mega-region
An expanding urban cluster, formed as a result of the convergence of more than one metropolitan/urban area.
megacity
In the context of Geography 11-12 and Geography 11-12 Life Skills, a megacity is a metropolitan area with a total population in excess of 10 million.
natural hazard
Atmospheric, hydrological and geomorphic processes and events in the environment that have the potential to damage the environment and endanger communities, e.g. bushfires, tropical cyclones, floods, earthquakes.
natural system
A combination of physical and/or biological elements, and their interconnected processes, that form an integrated whole.
oceanity
The effect of vast bodies of water on climatic conditions/patterns.
Overview Effect
A shift in awareness reported by some astronauts who have viewed the Earth from space, including the wonder of the Earth, the thinness of its atmosphere and the absence of national boundaries.
primary data
Original material collected e.g. field notes, measurements, responses to a survey or questionnaire.
range of scales
The varying frames of reference used for viewing and analysing the world in spatial terms. These are commonly described as local, national, regional and global. These scales can be used to develop an understanding of places, environments, natural and human phenomena, geographical patterns and processes, and management. They represent the geographical way of investigating the world.
regional centre
Places that have an economic and social role, servicing the area around them. They are often characterised by factors such as population growth, increasing population density and growth in employment.
remote settlement
A place that has less access to various goods and services because of its location and distance from larger urban settlements.
resilience
The ability of an ecosystem to tolerate and recover from natural and/or human-induced disturbance.
risk management
In the context of Geography 11-12 and Geography 11-12 Life Skills, risk management is defined in terms of preparedness, mitigation and/or prevention of a natural or ecological hazard. Preparedness involves planning the interventions needed to prevent or mitigate the effects of a hazard. Mitigation involves the implementation of strategies to eliminate or minimise the effects of these hazards. Adaptation involves adjusting to the changed environmental circumstances.
rural place/rural settlements
A reference to a settlement in the countryside typically serving a farming or agricultural area.
secondary sources/information
Sources of information that have been collected, processed, interpreted and published by others, e.g. census data, newspaper articles and images or information in a published report.
settlement pattern
The spatial distribution of different types of human settlement, e.g. dwellings, towns, cities. Settlements may take a range of forms, including linear, scattered, clustered, nucleated, dispersed, planned, radial.
shifting baselines
A theory that describes the way changes to an ecosystem are measured against previous reference points or baselines, which themselves may represent changes from an even earlier state of the ecosystem.
It reflects the perception of each new generation that current conditions are the same as past conditions. An ecosystem may therefore be degraded successively over time, so that the extent of change from its original state, is greater than perceived. Shifting baselines describes the situation where knowledge is lost about the original state of the natural world.
sociocultural
Related to social and cultural factors that influence people and communities.
spatial change
Change in the location and arrangement of particular features, phenomena or activities across the surface of the Earth.
spatial distribution
The location and arrangement of particular features, phenomena or activities across the surface of the Earth.
spatial technologies
Any form of technology that interacts with place, space and location. Spatial technologies may be used to collect, organise, record, visualise, manipulate, analyse and display spatial data.
spheres of influence
The geographical area over which the services and functions of an urban settlement extend. Larger settlements typically have a greater sphere of influence than smaller settlements and attract people from a wider geographical area.
students investigate
In the context of Geography 11-12, this encompasses a broad range of learning approaches and activities, facilitated by a variety of teaching strategies.
tipping points
A critical point (often called a threshold) where a series of smaller changes become significant enough, collectively, to trigger a larger-scale change. The change is often abrupt and irreversible, permanently altering the state of the original system, leading to flow-on effects that have more widespread consequences for other natural systems, and for people.
transformation
In the context of this syllabus, the term transformation refers to the processes of change from which forms of environmental, social, cultural and economic relationships and patterns emerge.
urban corridor
A part of an urban area defined by a particular geographical feature such as a transportation route.
urban hierarchy
The ranking of urban places in descending order, e.g. cities, determined by population size.
urban place
A reference to a permanent settlement or built-up area with a relatively dense population.
urbanisation
The process of demographic, economic and/or social change in which an increasing proportion of the population of a country or region live in urban areas. The size and rate of urbanisation is influenced by push-pull factors.
vulnerability
In the context of this syllabus, the term vulnerability refers to an ecosystem's risk of exposure or susceptibility to stress.

Global Terms
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples
Aboriginal Peoples are the first peoples of Australia and are represented by more than 250 language groups, each associated with a particular Country or territory. Torres Strait Islander Peoples are represented by 5 major island groups, and are associated with island territories to the north of Australia's Cape York which were annexed by Queensland in 1879. 
An Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander person is someone who: 
* is of Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander descent 
* identifies as an Aboriginal person and/or Torres Strait Islander person, and 
* is accepted as such by the Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander community(ies) in which they live.
Aboriginal English
A recognised dialect of English which is the first, or home language, of many Aboriginal people. It differs from other dialects of English, such as Standard Australian English, in systematic ways including sounds, grammar, words and their meanings, and language use. Aboriginal English is a powerful vehicle for the expression of Aboriginal identity. Aboriginal English is not a target language study option for NSW Aboriginal Languages syllabuses.
Aboriginal Songline(s)
Texts that describe landscapes and directions of the tracks forged in lands, waters and skies by Creator Spirits during the Dreaming.
accessibility
The extent to which a system, environment or object may be used irrespective of a user's capabilities or abilities. For example, the use of assistive technologies (AT) to allow people with disability to use computer systems, or the use of icons in place of words to allow young children to use a system.
assistive technology (AT)
A device or system whose primary purpose is to maintain or improve an individual's functioning and independence to facilitate participation and enhance overall wellbeing. This includes technologies specifically designed to meet an individual's needs, eg eye gaze technology, as well as more general technologies that can be used by anyone, eg speech-to-text applications. Assistive technology can also be referred to as inclusive technology.
augmentative and alternative communication (AAC)
An umbrella term that encompasses the communication methods used to supplement or replace speech or writing. AAC can be unaided, such as gestures, body language and sign language, or aided such as pictures, symbols, objects or speech generating devices.
See communication systems
communication forms
The ways people communicate and the communicative behaviours they use. Communication forms can be non-symbolic and/or symbolic. Non-symbolic forms include sounds, gestures, facial expressions and eye movements. Symbolic forms can be aided or non-aided. Aided forms of symbolic communication include objects, symbols, photographs and drawings. Aided forms can be digital. Non-aided forms of symbolic communication include formal gestures; speech; and signs, such as Key Word Sign.
copyright
The protection provided to the creators of original works and makers of sound recordings and films, that offers a legal framework for the control and reproduction or transmission of their literary, dramatic, artistic or musical works.
Country/Place
Country is used to describe a specific area of a nation or clan including physical, linguistic and spiritual features. Aboriginal communities' cultural associations with their Country may include or relate to languages, cultural practices, knowledge, songs, stories, art, paths, landforms, flora, fauna and minerals. These cultural associations may include custodial relationships with particular landscapes such as land, sea, sky, rivers as well as the intangible places associated with the Dreaming(s). Custodial relationships are extremely important in determining who may have the capacity to authentically speak for their Country. 
Place is a space mapped out by physical or intangible boundaries that individuals or groups of Torres Strait Islander Peoples occupy and regard as their own. It is a space with varying degrees of spirituality.
culture
The customs, habits, beliefs/spirituality, social organisation and ways of life that characterise different groups and communities. Cultural characteristics give a group or individual a sense of who they are and help them make sense of the world in which they live. Culture is a shared system but inherently diverse - there can be individual and group differences within cultures. Everyone has culture - it is a lens through which we see the world.
custodians
In Aboriginal communities, an individual charged with maintaining and passing on particular elements of cultural significance, eg language, stories, songs, rituals and imagery.
See owners
d/Deaf
When referring to deaf people who belong to a linguistic and cultural minority known as the Deaf community, the 'D' may be capitalised in reference to the individual, the group, or the culture in order to accord respect and deference, for example, the Deaf community. When referring simply to audiological status or when cultural affiliation is not known, as in the case of a person with a hearing loss in general, the lowercase 'd', as in 'deaf' is the more common usage.
Deaf
A cultural identity for people with hearing loss who share a common culture and who usually have a shared sign language.
disability
An umbrella term for any or all of the following components: 
* impairments: challenges in body function or structure
* activity limitations: difficulties in executing activities
* participation restrictions: challenges an individual may experience in involvement in life situations. (World Health Organization)
diversity
Differences that exist within a group, for example, age, sex, gender, gender expression, sexuality, ethnicity, ability/disability, body shape and composition, culture, religion/spirituality, learning differences, socioeconomic background, values and experiences.
Dreaming
The Dreaming has different meanings for different Aboriginal groups. The Dreaming can be seen as the embodiment of Aboriginal creation which gives meaning to everything; the essence of Aboriginal beliefs about creation and spiritual and physical existence. It establishes the rules governing relationships between the people, the land and all things for Aboriginal Peoples. The Dreaming is linked to the past, the present and the future. Where appropriate, refer to Aboriginal names for the Dreaming.
Elders
The custodians of knowledge and lore. They are chosen and accepted by their own communities as people who have the permission to disclose cultural knowledge and beliefs. Recognised Elders are highly respected people within Aboriginal communities. Proper consultation with local Aboriginal communities will often direct schools to recognised Elders.
first language(s)
The first language(s) that a person learns to speak.
Indigenous
An internationally recognised term for the first peoples of a land. In NSW the term Aboriginal person/Peoples is preferred.
See Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples
Indigenous cultural and intellectual property (ICIP)
Includes, but is not limited to, objects, sites, cultural knowledge, cultural expression and the arts, that have been transmitted or continue to be transmitted through generations as belonging to a particular Indigenous group or Indigenous people as a whole or their territory. 
See intellectual property
intellectual property
Non-material assets such as forms of cultural expression that belong to a particular individual or community. Intellectual property rights refer to the rights that the law grants to individuals for the protection of creative, intellectual, scientific and industrial activity, such as inventions. 
See Indigenous cultural and intellectual property (ICIP), and copyright
Key Word Sign
A communication strategy that incorporates signing with speech. It is used to support language development for people with communication difficulties. Although Key Word Sign uses a simplified form of manual signing, it is different to Auslan, as it is not a signed language.
kinship
A key aspect of Aboriginal cultures and values. It includes the importance of all relationships and of being related to and belonging to the land.
language group
An Aboriginal community identified with a common language, both verbal and nonverbal, and with a particular territory. Used in preference to the term 'tribe'.
language revival
The process and range of strategies for increasing knowledge and use of a language that is no longer spoken fluently across all generations in the context of language loss or language dispossession caused by colonisation. Aboriginal Languages and Torres Strait Islander Languages are being revived through community initiatives, linguistic research and school programs. 'Language revival' may be used as an overarching term that could also include 'reclamation', 'revitalisation', 'renewal' and 'reawakening'.
local Aboriginal community
A local Aboriginal community is constituted by those people who are Aboriginal and who reside in the near locality. Aboriginal communities will have a rich and diverse history that has been seriously affected by dispossession and relations, which sees families with spiritual connection to Country residing beside those who have been forced to move from other locations. The notion of locality is complex and multilayered: schools should seek advice from a range of people and/or organisations representing local interests. 
See community(ies)
off-Country
Taking place away from Aboriginal land or Country of origin. 
See Country/Place
on-Country
Taking place on Aboriginal land or Country of origin.
See Country/Place
owners
Each Aboriginal Language is recognised as belonging to a particular geographical area and thus to the people who can claim a connection to that area. Aboriginal community members acquire ownership of their language(s) at birth. Language proficiency is not essential for ownership.
See custodians
protocols
The appropriate ways of behaving, communicating and showing respect for diversity of history and culture. This involves appreciation of the knowledge, standing and status of people within the local Aboriginal community and the school community. Protocols inevitably vary between communities, and between people within a community. In establishing a partnership between schools and Aboriginal communities, it is especially important that protocols are acknowledged and respected.
respect
A term used commonly in NSW Aboriginal communities to refer to the way an individual treats others. Showing respect occurs in many ways, such as waiting to speak, listening and demonstrating understanding, not asking too many direct questions, ensuring that people are not made to feel uncomfortable or uneasy, and generally showing regard for others' ideas, beliefs and culture.
sign
Hand signs (or hand talk) used to supplement or replace oral language. Signs form part of nonverbal communication for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples and may be used by people who are hearing, or d/Deaf or hard of hearing. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Sign Languages may be used in some areas. Some Sign Languages may be associated with sacred ceremonial practices.
signage
The use of words, graphic designs and/or symbols used to communicate a message, eg information signs, plaques, warning signs, road signs, signs that show direction.
switch
A type of assistive technology that enables people with cognitive and/or physical disability to access a range of devices, including computers and communication devices. Switches can be activated by touch, or triggered without contact, such as through eye gaze, sound or blowing.
See assistive technology (AT)
yarning circle
Yarning circles are an important cultural practice for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples to learn within the collective group. Knowledge and information are shared in harmony and respect with all individuals.
